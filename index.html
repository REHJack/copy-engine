The Editor and Highlight Toggles

Paste any copy into the editor and Copy Engine analyses it immediately. No button to press — the tool works in real time as you type or paste.

Six types of issue are highlighted using colour coding. Use the toggle pills above the editor to switch any highlight type on or off. This is useful when you want to focus on one problem at a time without visual noise from the others.

ColourWhat it flags
RedPassive voice
YellowFiller words
OrangeLong sentences (30+ words)
PurpleClichés
BlueRepeated words
PinkSentence variety issues

Click any highlighted word or phrase to see a tooltip explaining what the issue is and how to fix it.

Active Voice Score

A percentage score from 0 to 100% that measures how direct and active your writing is. The higher the score, the cleaner the copy.

How it is calculated

The tool counts passive voice constructions and filler words across your text, then applies a weighted penalty based on how frequently they appear relative to your total word count. Passive phrases carry a heavier penalty than filler words, because passive voice has a greater impact on clarity and reader engagement.

ScoreRatingWhat it means
Above 85%ExcellentLean and direct — your copy is working hard
65 to 85%GoodA few fixes will sharpen it further
Below 65%Needs workToo much passive voice or filler dragging it down
Why it matters: Active copy is easier to read, more persuasive, and feels more confident. "We reduced costs by 30%" lands harder than "Costs were reduced by 30%."
🔴 Passive Voice

Passive voice occurs when the subject of a sentence receives the action rather than performing it. "The report was written by Sarah" is passive. "Sarah wrote the report" is active.

How it is detected

Copy Engine uses pattern matching to identify common passive constructions — forms of "to be" (is, was, were, been, being) followed by a past participle such as written, approved, or delivered.

How to fix it

The tooltip on each flagged phrase will usually suggest an active alternative. The general fix is to ask: who is doing this? Then put them at the front of the sentence.

Why it matters: Passive voice buries the subject, weakens the verb, and makes copy feel corporate and distant. Marketing copy almost always performs better when it is direct and action-led.
🟡 Filler Words

Filler words add length but not meaning. Words like just, really, very, quite, essentially, actually, basically and similar weaken the sentences they appear in.

How it is detected

The tool matches against a curated list of filler words. Any match in your copy is flagged. Brand Terms you have added are exempt from this check.

Quick fix: The Fix Fillers button in the top bar removes all detected filler words in one click. Use it as a starting point, then review — occasionally context matters and a filler word is genuinely needed.
Why it matters: Every unnecessary word is a moment where a reader can disengage. Tighter copy converts better.
🟠 Long Sentences

Any sentence of 30 words or more is flagged in orange.

How it is detected

The tool splits your copy into individual sentences and counts the words in each. Sentences at or above the 30-word threshold are highlighted.

How to fix it

Look for conjunctions such as and, but, because, which, that — these are often natural split points. One idea per sentence is a reliable rule of thumb.

Why it matters: Long sentences are harder to process, especially on screen. Web readers scan rather than read. Short sentences hold attention better and improve comprehension across all audiences.
🟣 Clichés

Overused phrases that have lost their impact — hit the ground running, game-changer, move the needle, best-in-class and so on.

How it is detected

Matched against a library of known marketing clichés. The tooltip on each flagged phrase suggests a more specific or literal alternative.

Why it matters: Clichés signal lazy writing and erode trust. When a phrase has been used thousands of times, it no longer creates any impression on the reader. Specificity beats vague superlatives every time.
🔵 Repetition

Words used multiple times in close proximity, creating a noticeable pattern that disrupts reading flow.

How it is detected

The tool uses character-proximity detection, looking for the same word appearing within a defined window of surrounding text. Common short words and stop words are excluded automatically. Words added as Brand Terms are also excluded. Topic words — those spread broadly and consistently throughout the copy — are distinguished from genuine repetition clusters, so the tool flags localised overuse rather than penalising words that appear throughout a longer piece by design.

Why it matters: Repeated words make copy feel unpolished and narrow. Varied vocabulary signals craft and keeps readers engaged.
🥰 Sentence Variety

Flags groups of consecutive sentences that start with the same word — for example, three sentences in a row beginning with "We".

How it is detected

The tool checks the opening word of each sentence and identifies runs of two or more sentences with identical starts. These are highlighted pink in the editor. The sidebar panel shows a breakdown of your sentence length distribution — short, medium, and long — so you can see whether your copy has a healthy rhythm or leans too heavily in one direction.

Why it matters: Copy where every sentence starts the same way creates a monotonous rhythm that loses readers. A mix of sentence structures and openings — and a balance of short and long sentences — gives copy energy and flow. A piece composed entirely of short sentences can feel choppy; entirely long ones can feel exhausting.
Readability Score

A plain-English label describing how easy or difficult your copy is to read, ranging from Very Easy through to Very Difficult.

How it is calculated

Using the Flesch Reading Ease Score (FRES) formula:

FRES = 206.835 − (1.015 × average sentence length) − (84.6 × average syllables per word)

The higher the score, the easier the read. The formula weights syllable complexity more heavily than sentence length, because vocabulary complexity is a stronger predictor of reading difficulty than sentence structure alone.

ScoreLabelTypical use
80 to 100Very EasyBroad consumer campaigns
70 to 79EasyEmail marketing, landing pages
60 to 69Fairly EasyBlogs, social copy
50 to 59StandardGeneral B2B content
40 to 49Fairly DifficultProfessional and trade content
30 to 39DifficultTechnical or academic audiences
Below 30Very DifficultSpecialist and legal copy
Why it matters: Matching your readability level to your audience is one of the most overlooked factors in copy performance. Consumer-facing copy that scores in the Difficult range will lose readers before they convert.
Audience Matcher

Scores your copy against a chosen audience profile — General Public, Consumer, Professional, C-Suite, Technical, or Academic.

How it is calculated

Three factors are combined into a single score out of 100:

FactorWeightWhat it measures
Readability40 pointsHow well your Flesch score aligns with the target audience's expected reading level
Sentence length30 pointsHow close your average sentence length is to the ideal for that audience
Issue rate30 pointsHow few passive voice and filler word problems your copy contains
Why it matters: A piece of copy that scores well overall might still be wrong for the audience you are writing for. A C-Suite piece should read differently to a consumer email. This score tells you whether the register is right, not just whether the copy is clean.
Tone Detection

Identifies the dominant tone of your copy from a set of profiles: Conversational, Formal, Promotional, Technical, or Neutral.

How it is detected

The tool analyses vocabulary patterns. Contractions, short sentences, and informal words suggest a Conversational tone. Domain-specific terminology suggests Technical. Words like offer, save, exclusive suggest Promotional, and so on. The profile with the strongest signal match is shown.

Why it matters: Tone mismatch is one of the most common issues in marketing copy. A formal tone in a consumer campaign, or a promotional tone in a technical white paper, undermines credibility.
SEO Keywords

A suite of tools for checking how well your copy is optimised for search. The panel covers primary keyword density and placement, secondary keyword coverage, and a breakdown of the most frequently used meaningful words in your copy.

Primary Keyword

Enter your main target keyword. The tool shows density (how often it appears as a percentage of total words), placement (whether it appears within the first 100 words — a strong SEO signal), and a combined Focus Score.

Density targets adjust automatically based on your content type, because the right keyword density for a 200-word ad is different to a 2,000-word article. Short-form copy calls for a higher percentage; long-form copy requires a lower one to avoid appearing repetitive.

StatusWhat it means
Too LowNot enough mentions — search engines may not associate the page with this term
OptimalIn the recommended range for your content type
Too HighOveruse detected — risks appearing as keyword stuffing
Secondary Keywords

Add up to 5 supporting keywords. The tool checks whether each one appears in your copy and calculates a Depth Score — how many of your secondary keywords are present and accounted for.

Why secondary keywords matter: Search engines reward topical coverage, not just primary keyword repetition. Secondary keywords demonstrate that your content covers the subject comprehensively.
Top Keywords Found

Automatically surfaces the five most frequently used meaningful words in your copy. Useful for checking whether your copy is actually about what you think it is about — and for spotting unintentional keyword overuse.

Brand Terms

A session-based list of words and phrases that should never be flagged as filler words, clichés, or repetition. Add any brand names, product names, or proprietary terms and the tool will ignore them when scanning for issues.

Important: Brand Terms are not saved between sessions, because the tool is used across multiple clients and projects. You will need to re-add them each time you open the tool.
CTA Detection

Detects whether your copy contains a call to action, matched against a library of common CTA phrases such as get started, book a demo, sign up, find out more, download and so on.

The tool indicates whether you have one or two CTAs. A single CTA is fine. Multiple can reinforce, but too many can dilute the message and confuse the reader about what to do next.

Why it matters: Copy without a CTA is copy without a purpose. Even if the writing is strong, a missing or weak call to action leaves readers with nowhere to go.
Version Diff

A comparison tool that shows exactly what changed between two versions of your copy. Paste an earlier version into the Version Diff panel, then click Compare Versions.

Added words are highlighted in green and removed words in red. A score delta shows whether your edits improved or reduced the Active Voice Score, so you can see at a glance whether the revision moved in the right direction.

Why it matters: Editing is iterative. The diff tool makes it easy to review changes without losing track of what you changed and why.
Copy Audit

A consolidated view of all detected issues in your copy — passive voice, fillers, clichés, long sentences, and repetition — listed as individual issue cards. Each card shows the problematic phrase, an explanation of why it is an issue, a specific fix suggestion, and a Show me button that jumps the editor directly to that location.

Click the Audit button in the top bar to open the panel. It slides out below the editor and can be closed at any time.

Note: The Copy Audit runs entirely in your browser. No internet connection is required and no data is sent anywhere.
Saved Drafts and Folders

A history system for saving, organising, and retrieving previous versions of your copy. Create folders to organise work by client or project, then save entries into them. Each saved entry records the copy, score, date, and time. Click any entry to reload it into the editor.

How to use it

Click New Folder in the top bar to create a folder. Use the Save button to save the current copy into a folder. Switch between folders using the tabs that appear below the top bar.

Important: Drafts are saved in your browser's local storage. They are specific to the browser and device you are using. Clearing your browser data will permanently remove all saved drafts.
Export: PDF and HTML
PDF Export

Produces a formatted copy audit document including the editor content, scores, and sidebar analysis. Useful for sharing with clients or keeping as a record of a piece at a particular point in time.

HTML Export

Downloads a standalone copy of the entire tool, including your current copy and settings. The file can be opened in any browser without an internet connection, making it useful for archiving or sharing a working snapshot of the tool.
